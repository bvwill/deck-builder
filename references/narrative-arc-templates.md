# Narrative Arc Templates

Most presentations fail at the structural level, not the content level. The content is fine. The story isn't there. This reference gives you six proven story structures -- drawn from Pixar, screenwriting theory, and narrative psychology -- and maps each one to the presentation contexts where it works.

The thesis: **every memorable presentation is a story with a spine.** The spine determines whether the audience follows. The content fills in what happens along the spine.

---

## How to Use This

Before structuring any deck, answer two questions:
1. What do I want the audience to *feel* at the end? (Not "understand" -- *feel.*)
2. What is the fundamental tension this talk lives in?

The answer to #2 points you to the right arc. The answer to #1 shapes how you close.

## The Six Arcs

---

### 1. The Pixar Story Spine

**Origin:** Emma Coats, Pixar storyboard artist, 2011 -- rooted in improv (Kenn Adams).

**The spine:**
- *Once upon a time...* -- establish the world as it was
- *Every day...* -- show the normal pattern
- *Until one day...* -- the inciting disruption
- *Because of that...* -- consequence (repeat 1-3x, each escalating)
- *Until finally...* -- the resolution
- *Ever since then...* -- the new normal; what changed

**Why it works:** The "Because of that... because of that..." chain creates causality. Audiences track cause-and-effect far more easily than they track lists of facts. Each link in the chain demands the next. You can't stop listening.

**Best for:** Before/after transformation stories. Case studies. Customer journeys. Product launches where the narrative has a clear turning point.

**Presentation map:**
- Sales pitch: *"Once upon a time, our customers were managing this with spreadsheets. Every day, they lost 4 hours to reconciliation. Until one day, they tried [product]. Because of that, the team got those hours back. Because of that, they could actually focus on strategy. Until finally, they closed 40% more deals. Ever since then, they've never looked back."*
- The spine can run in 90 seconds or anchor a 30-minute keynote. Scale by expanding each beat.

### 2. The Hero's Journey (Monomyth)

**Origin:** Joseph Campbell, *The Hero with a Thousand Faces* (1949). Condensed to 12 stages by Christopher Vogler.

**The spine (condensed to 5 moves for presentations):**
1. **Ordinary World** -- the hero (speaker, company, customer) in their current state
2. **The Call** -- something disrupts the status quo; a problem or opportunity appears
3. **Crossing the Threshold** -- they commit to change; they enter unfamiliar territory
4. **The Ordeal** -- the hardest moment; the real test; the near-failure
5. **Return with the Elixir** -- transformed; with something of value to share

**Why it works:** Humans are wired for this structure. It appears in virtually every culture's mythology. The Ordeal is critical -- without genuine struggle, there's no earned resolution. Audiences don't believe in victories that weren't fought for.

**Best for:** Founder stories. Keynotes. Investor pitches where you need to establish credibility through adversity. Origin stories. Change-management narratives.

**The failure mode:** Skipping the Ordeal. Many founders tell "Ordinary World → Call → Return with Elixir" and skip the suffering. The audience doesn't buy it. The Ordeal is what makes the transformation believable.

**Presentation map:**
- *Ordinary World:* "Two years ago I was running three agencies simultaneously with a team of 40 and I was completely underwater."
- *Call:* "I started using AI tools out of desperation."
- *Threshold:* "I cut the team from 40 to 4."
- *Ordeal:* "For three months nothing worked. I almost quit."
- *Elixir:* "Then I built a system. Revenue per person went up 8x. That's what I'm teaching today."

### 3. Man in a Hole (Vonnegut)

**Origin:** Kurt Vonnegut's rejected University of Chicago thesis (1950s) -- he mapped story shapes on axes of happiness (Y) vs. time (X).

**The spine:**
1. **Baseline** -- character is in a reasonably good place
2. **The Hole** -- they fall into misfortune; something goes badly wrong
3. **Struggle** -- they try to get out; it doesn't work at first
4. **Climb** -- they find a way; things begin to improve
5. **New Baseline** -- they return to good (often slightly above where they started)

**Why it works:** It's the most satisfying arc emotionally. The audience experiences relief and resolution. The "hole" creates stakes; the climb creates investment; the resolution delivers payoff.

**Vonnegut's insight:** The new baseline should be *slightly higher* than the original, not dramatically higher. Cinderella-style overcorrection feels unearned. A modest, believable improvement after genuine struggle is more resonant.

**Best for:** Crisis communications. Turnaround stories. Board reviews that need to acknowledge a hard quarter before showing recovery. Any talk where transparency about failure is a credibility move.

**Presentation map:**
- *Baseline:* "In Q1 we were hitting plan."
- *Hole:* "In Q2 we lost our two largest customers in 6 weeks. It was bad."
- *Struggle:* "We tried [approach A]. It didn't work. We tried [approach B]. Also didn't work."
- *Climb:* "In October we made a hard call: [decision]. We cut [X]. We doubled down on [Y]."
- *New Baseline:* "Q4 retention is back to 94%. The team is smaller but sharper. Here's what we learned."

### 4. The Quest

**Origin:** Christopher Booker, *The Seven Basic Plots* (2004). One of the seven universal story patterns.

**The spine:**
1. **The Call** -- a problem, goal, or prize is identified; it feels impossible from here
2. **Departure** -- the journey begins; early optimism
3. **The Road** -- a series of tests, each revealing what's needed to succeed
4. **The Ordeal** -- the climactic challenge; the point where everything is at stake
5. **The Prize** -- the goal achieved; often with unexpected wisdom gained alongside it

**Why it works:** Quest structures work when the destination is clear from the start -- the tension is *whether they'll make it*, not *where they're going*. The audience tracks progress toward a visible finish line.

**Best for:** Product roadmaps. OKR reviews. Fundraising pitches. Mission-driven talks where the goal is ambitious and the path is uncertain.

**Presentation map:**
- *Call:* "We want to be the infrastructure layer for AI-assisted PM work. That's the prize."
- *Departure:* "We started with one tool, one customer."
- *Road:* "We built the evaluation layer. Then the skills layer. Each one was harder than expected."
- *Ordeal:* "Six months in, we almost ran out of runway. We had to cut two features and bet on one."
- *Prize:* "That bet worked. Today 400 PMs use it weekly. Here's what the next 18 months look like."

### 5. Rebirth

**Origin:** Christopher Booker, *The Seven Basic Plots*. The transformation arc.

**The spine:**
1. **Stuck** -- the protagonist is trapped in a limiting pattern, belief, or situation
2. **The Shadow** -- a force (internal or external) deepens the trap
3. **The Catalyst** -- something breaks through; a person, idea, or event forces change
4. **Transformation** -- the protagonist sheds the old self and becomes new
5. **Emergence** -- the new self steps into the world; the old limitation is gone

**Why it works:** Rebirth is the most *personal* arc. It works when the speaker or subject has genuinely changed. Audiences recognize authentic transformation. It creates permission for the audience to imagine their own change.

**Best for:** Coaching/teaching contexts. Testimonials. Any talk about mindset shift, behavior change, or category creation. "I used to believe X, now I know Y" structures.

**Presentation map (course/workshop):**
- *Stuck:* "Most PMs think they can't code. They've been told that. They believe it."
- *Shadow:* "So they stay dependent on engineers for everything. Their leverage never grows."
- *Catalyst:* "But Claude Code doesn't require coding. It requires thinking like a PM."
- *Transformation:* "In 4 weeks, students go from zero to building their own tools."
- *Emergence:* "Not as developers. As PMs who can build. That distinction matters."

### 6. Problem-Agitate-Solution (PAS)

**Origin:** Copywriting and sales psychology. Dan Kennedy popularized it; it's one of the oldest persuasion frameworks.

**The spine:**
1. **Problem** -- name the specific pain, plainly and without embellishment
2. **Agitate** -- deepen the stakes; show what the problem costs, blocks, or destroys
3. **Solution** -- introduce the fix; show how it resolves the specific pain

**Why it works:** PAS works on *relevance*. The audience only cares about your solution if they've first felt the weight of the problem. Agitation is the emotional activation that makes the solution land as relief, not just information.

**The rule on agitation:** You earn the right to agitate by naming the problem accurately first. If the audience nods at the problem statement, they'll lean in during agitation. If they squint (because you've overstated), you've lost them before the solution.

**Best for:** Sales pitches. Ad-style presentations. Short, punchy formats (lightning lessons, investor updates, sales one-pagers turned into decks). Any context where you need to move fast and the audience needs to feel urgency.

**Presentation map:**
- *Problem:* "You spend 6 hours a week in meetings that exist to recap last week's meetings."
- *Agitate:* "That's 300 hours a year. That's 7 full work weeks. You'll never get those back. And the people in those meetings aren't your best people -- your best people found ways to avoid them."
- *Solution:* "Async briefings, built by Claude, sent before anyone opens Slack. Here's how."

## Quick Selector

| Situation | Best Arc |
|---|---|
| Customer transformation / case study | Pixar Story Spine |
| Founder / origin story | Hero's Journey |
| Turnaround / hard quarter / crisis | Man in a Hole |
| Roadmap / OKR review / ambitious goal | The Quest |
| Coaching / mindset shift / category creation | Rebirth |
| Sales pitch / urgent problem / short format | Problem-Agitate-Solution |
| Investor pitch | Hero's Journey or The Quest |
| Board review | Man in a Hole (if recovering) or The Quest (if forward-looking) |
| Workshop / course | Rebirth or Pixar Story Spine |
| Competitive positioning talk | PAS (the competitor is the Problem) |

---

## Combining Arcs

Real presentations often layer two arcs:
- **Hero's Journey + PAS:** The hero's call-to-adventure is the problem; the ordeal is the agitation; the elixir is the solution.
- **Man in a Hole + Quest:** The hole is the problem; the quest is the climb.
- **Rebirth + Pixar Spine:** The transformation moment is the "Until one day..."; the emergence is "Ever since then."

The combination to avoid: **two arcs with different emotional registers** in the same talk. Rebirth (personal, intimate) and Swiss-logic PAS (fast, transactional) fight each other. Pick a dominant register and keep it.

---

## A Note on the Pixar Storyteller's Core Principle

Pixar's most important story rule isn't a structure -- it's a sequence:

> *You admire a character for trying more than for their successes.*

Translate this for presentations: **audiences trust you for the struggle, not the win.** The speaker who shares the Ordeal honestly is more credible than the one who only shows the elixir. Don't skip the hard part.
