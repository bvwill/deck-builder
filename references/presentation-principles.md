# Presentation Principles

These are not design tips. They are the psychological mechanics behind why some presentations change how a person thinks and most don't. Apply these when structuring content, choosing density, and deciding what earns a slide.

The core thesis: **a deck is not a document. A document can be understood without you in the room. A presentation cannot -- and shouldn't try.** The moment you design a deck to be self-explanatory, you've built a handout. Slides are props for a speaker, or (for async) a scaffold for ideas -- not a substitute for both.

---

## Principle 1: Image + Voice. Never Text + Voice.

**The mechanism.**
The brain has two separate channels for processing information: visual (spatial, image-based) and auditory/verbal (language-based). When you show an image and speak, both channels work simultaneously -- double the processing capacity. When you put text on screen and speak, you force the audience to choose: read (visual channel processing language) or listen (auditory channel processing language). Both channels compete for the same resource. One loses.

This is Richard Mayer's **Multimedia Learning Theory**, specifically the Redundancy Effect and the Split-Attention Effect:
- **Redundancy:** Text on screen + identical narration = the audience processes the same information twice through the wrong channels. Recall drops.
- **Split attention:** Text on screen + different narration = the audience can't follow either fully.

**The rule.**
One image (or one concept visualized) per slide. The speaker carries the narrative. The slide is an anchor, not a transcript.

If you read text on a slide aloud, delete the text.

**When text IS appropriate.**
- Labels and data markers on a chart (these are *reference* text, not narration)
- A name, URL, or contact info the audience needs to copy
- A short quote in the speaker's voice (audience reads it *as* the speaker reads it -- synchronized, not competing)
- Accessibility layer (live captioning context)

**Failure mode.**
Slides that look like presenter notes. Dense bullet lists that the presenter "covers" by reading them aloud. The audience is already three bullets ahead of you, half-listening, half-reading, retaining neither.

## Principle 2: Contrast Earns Attention. Linearity Loses It.

**The mechanism.**
Human attention is not sustained by interest -- it's triggered by *change and deviation*. The brain's salience detection fires on unexpected transitions, not steady-state information. A deck structured as "here's point 1, then point 2, then point 3" gives the brain no deviation signal to latch onto. A deck structured as "here's the world as it is → here's what's broken about it → here's what it could be" fires that signal on every transition.

This is Nancy Duarte's **Sparkline** structure: oscillate between *what is* (current reality) and *what could be* (desired future). Each oscillation is a micro-tension the audience wants resolved.

**The structure.**
3-5 oscillations in a talk is the research-backed sweet spot (Duarte's analysis of Jobs keynotes):
1. Establish the status quo -- name the current reality without judgment
2. Introduce the tension -- what's broken, limiting, or costly about it
3. Plant the possibility -- glimpse of what it could be
4. Show evidence -- proof the possibility is real
5. Close the gap -- land the future state as the new reality

Each oscillation needs 20-30 seconds of *held tension* before the release. Rushing the resolution kills the emotional response.

**What makes contrast land vs. feel manipulative.**
Contrast lands when the "what is" is the audience's actual lived reality -- not a strawman, not exaggerated. State the current state plainly, even neutrally, before you make it feel urgent. If your "what is" requires loaded language to sound bad, you've manufactured the contrast rather than revealed it.

Authenticity test: Can you state the current reality in a sentence your audience would say themselves?

**Failure modes.**
- One before/after (single oscillation): feels like a pitch, not a narrative
- Agitation without authenticity: feels cynical
- "What could be" without a path to get there: inspirational-porn -- moving in the moment, irrelevant by next morning

## Principle 3: Engineer One Peak. Protect It.

**The mechanism.**
Kahneman's **Peak-End Rule**: memory doesn't record experiences as video. It records two snapshots -- the most intense moment (the peak) and the ending. A 45-minute presentation that's consistently good but peaks at "fine" will be remembered as "fine." A 20-minute talk with one shattering moment and a landing close will be remembered as "that talk."

Duration is irrelevant. Middle-quality is irrelevant. Only peak and end matter.

**How to build a peak.**
A peak requires three elements:
1. **Setup** -- establish stakes for long enough that the revelation has context. The iPhone reveal worked because Jobs spent 10 minutes explaining how locked-down mobile software was.
2. **Revelation** -- the moment of surprise, recognition, or awe. New data that lands differently because of the setup. A demo that does what they didn't know was possible. A silence.
3. **Sensory anchor** -- something that locks the peak into memory. Jobs' pause after "This is iPhone." Hormozi's shocking stat stated once, slowly. The physical object (MacBook Air from manila envelope). Silence as punctuation.

**Peak placement.**
Research supports placing the peak at 60-70% through the talk. Early enough that the close can breathe; late enough that the audience has been primed.

**What kills peaks.**
- **Abundance:** If every fifth slide is "THIS IS THE IMPORTANT PART" -- nothing is. Scarcity creates peaks. When everything is urgent, nothing is.
- **Missing the setup:** A shocking stat with no context is just a number. The same stat after three minutes of establishing why this problem costs $XM is a revelation.
- **Weak endings:** A peak followed by "so, yeah, any questions?" undoes the work. The end is 50% of what they remember.

**Peak vs. gimmick.**
A gimmick impresses in the moment but has no content weight -- a flashy animation, a surprise video that's tangentially related, a prop that doesn't connect to the idea. A peak is the moment the audience will reference afterward when they describe the talk to someone else.

## Principle 4: Three Ideas. Anchor Them to Something Held.

**The mechanism.**
Miller's Law: working memory holds ~7 discrete items. But "items" is a loaded word. Chunking collapses many facts into one meaningful unit. "The three drivers of churn" is one item, not three -- if the audience understands the frame. Chunking doesn't reduce information; it reduces the *number of independent things the audience must track.*

The Rule of Three specifically: two concepts = coincidence, three = pattern. The brain recognizes "this is structured" at three and allocates deeper processing. Four or more starts to feel like a list. Lists aren't remembered; structures are.

**The rule.**
No more than 3 primary ideas in any talk. No more than 3 supporting points per idea. If you have 5 things to cover, find the 2 that are actually sub-points of one of the 3.

**Metaphors as memory anchors.**
A metaphor works by activating an existing schema in the audience's brain and mapping new information onto it. When you say "debugging is like detective work," the audience retrieves their detective schema (clues, elimination, hypothesis testing) and uses it to hold the unfamiliar concept. The key is *structural* analogy, not surface similarity.

Good analogy: "A customer success team is like an immune system -- it recognizes problems early, neutralizes them locally, and signals the body when something is systemic." (Deep structural overlap.)

Bad analogy: "Our roadmap is like a garden." (Vague surface similarity, no structural mapping.)

If you give the audience a metaphor, framework name, or acronym to hold, they'll remember the structure long after the content fades.

**Failure mode.**
Three buckets with 6-7 sub-items each -- the top-level structure drowns. Over-abstracted buckets ("efficiency, effectiveness, engagement") that sound organized but carry no anchor. Competing frameworks in one talk ("use the 3 Ps, also remember the 4 Cs") that the audience can't hold simultaneously.

## Principle 5: Density Is the Enemy. Hierarchy Is the Tool.

**The mechanism.**
Mayer's **Coherence Principle**: every element on a slide that doesn't directly support the core idea competes for the audience's attention. Not just slightly -- it actively degrades retention of everything else. Decorative elements, supplementary info, extra context -- all of it is cognitive interference, not decoration.

**Kosslyn's research on slide density:**
- Slides with 40%+ whitespace have 30% higher recall than dense slides
- 30-word slides are comprehensible; 50+ word slides lose the audience during the slide
- A slide with image + text + chart = three processing streams. One per slide is the ceiling.

**The visual hierarchy rules.**
- Two font sizes per slide maximum. Size + weight creates hierarchy; adding more sizes creates chaos.
- Font size ratio: 1:1.5 (e.g., 16px body, 24px headline). The golden ratio (1:1.618) feels natural. 1:2 creates visual shock.
- Weight carries more hierarchy signal than size alone. Inter Regular at 16px vs. Inter Bold at 16px is a clear hierarchy without a size jump.
- High contrast (dark on light, or light on dark) activates salience. Low contrast slides past the brain.
- Line length: 30-40 characters per line for presentation slides. Anything longer, the eye is scanning, not reading.

**The whitespace rule.**
~40% of each slide should be empty. Not wasted -- *working*. Whitespace directs attention to what's there. Uncomfortably minimal slides force the audience to listen to the speaker. That's the goal.

**Typography choices carry meaning.**
- Sans-serif at heavy weights: clarity, directness, modernity (Swiss Rigor)
- Serif at light-to-medium weights: warmth, patience, authority (Editorial Warmth)
- Monospace: technical, precise, unadorned (code, data contexts)

**Failure mode.**
The "full briefing document" slide: every context, caveat, and supporting fact crammed onto one slide "so the audience has it." They won't read it. They'll see a wall and stop listening.

## Principle 6: Voice the "So What" -- Every Time.

**The mechanism.**
**Relevance Theory** (Sperber & Wilson): communication only succeeds when the audience understands *why this information is relevant to them*. Presenters assume relevance is obvious. It usually isn't. The audience's brain is constantly running a background evaluation: *is this worth tracking?* If it can't quickly answer yes, it stops tracking.

**Schema activation:** Every claim lands in context of an existing mental model. "We reduced churn by 40%" activates a *business health* schema. But the meaning -- what it *costs*, what it *enables*, what it *changes* -- is often implicit. Explicit connection makes the landing stick.

**Jobs vs. Hormozi.**
Both are masters of the "so what" -- but they use opposite approaches:

- **Jobs (implicit):** "A thousand songs in your pocket." Pause. He doesn't explain it. The audience *completes the thought themselves*: "I can have all my music everywhere." Participatory encoding -- the audience's own inference creates a deeper memory trace. Works when: high trust, sophisticated audience, strong shared context.

- **Hormozi (explicit and cascading):** Every claim gets a full consequence chain. "This is a high-ticket offer framework. Here's why: it lets you charge 3-5x more per customer, which means you need 10 customers instead of 100, which means less support overhead, which means more margin, which means you can reinvest in acquisition." He closes every gap. Works when: lower trust, more diverse audience, or higher stakes around comprehension.

**The rule.**
Every fact must have a "why this matters" attached -- spoken, if not written. If you can't articulate the "so what" for a piece of content, it probably doesn't earn its slide.

**The balance.**
Under-explaining: "We increased velocity by 18%." (Audience: so what?)
Over-explaining: "We increased velocity by 18%, which means we got faster, which is better than slower." (Audience: I'm not an idiot.)
Right: "We increased velocity by 18% -- which meant we shipped the features Q3 customers were waiting on, and that directly contributed to the retention uptick."

**Failure mode.**
Treating the "so what" as obvious. It almost never is -- not because the audience is slow, but because *they don't have your context*. You've been living with this for weeks. They heard it for the first time 90 seconds ago.

## Quick-Reference Checklist

Use this when reviewing a proposed deck structure before building.

**Content:**
- [ ] Three primary ideas max. Each anchored to a metaphor or structure.
- [ ] Every fact has an explicit "so what" attached -- either on slide or scripted in speaker notes.
- [ ] One peak identified. Setup is in place. Peak lands at 60-70% of the talk.
- [ ] The ending is designed, not defaulted to. Close = 1-2 punchy statements, not a summary.

**Structure:**
- [ ] At least 3 contrast oscillations (what is → what could be). Not a flat list.
- [ ] Section dividers create breathing room between major moves.
- [ ] Slide count matches the format: live = leaner, async = slightly more self-explanatory.

**Visual:**
- [ ] One primary concept per slide.
- [ ] 40%+ whitespace on dense slides.
- [ ] Two font sizes max.
- [ ] Text on slides is either a label, a quote, or a keyword -- not a sentence that will also be spoken.
- [ ] Contrast verified on all backgrounds.

**The final question before shipping:**
*If the presenter left the room and someone else had to narrate this deck, could they? If yes, there's too much text. The slides should only make sense with a speaker in the room.*
