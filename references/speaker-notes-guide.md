# Speaker Notes Guide

Speaker notes are where the deck becomes a presentation. They're the cognitive layer that connects the slide (the anchor) to the talk (the meaning). Written well, they make the difference between a speaker who reads slides and a speaker who *uses* slides.

The core principle: **notes complement the slide -- they never duplicate it.**

---

## What Notes Are For

Notes serve four functions, in order of importance:

1. **The "so what"** -- the explicit meaning of what's on screen. What the speaker says that the slide can't show.
2. **The transition** -- how you get from this slide to the next one. The connective tissue of the narrative.
3. **The prompt** -- the one thing the speaker needs to remember to say. Not a script; a trigger.
4. **The timing anchor** -- approximate time spent on this slide, for talks with a hard clock.

They do not serve as: a full script, a backup document for if you forget what to say, or a record of everything you know about this topic.

---

## The Four Note Types

### Type 1: The "So What" Note
Use when the slide shows data, a quote, a visual, or a fact -- and the meaning isn't self-evident.

Format: One sentence, starting with why it matters.

> *"This is the number that changed how we thought about the whole product -- it meant we were solving the wrong problem for two years."*

Not: *"This slide shows our Q3 NPS trend."* (That's what the slide shows. Notes aren't captions.)

---

### Type 2: The Transition Note
Use on every slide that moves from one idea to another. Transitions are the hardest thing to improvise and the easiest to pre-write.

Format: One sentence bridge, ending with the setup for what comes next.

> *"So now we know the problem is real -- here's what we almost got wrong when we tried to solve it."*

Transitions that create contrast (the Duarte oscillation) pull the audience forward. Transitions that just signal sequence ("Next, I'll talk about...") are dead air.

---

### Type 3: The Story Prompt
Use on slides where you're telling a story, not stating facts. The note is a headline for the story, not the story itself. You know the story -- you just need the door.

Format: Character + moment + what happened.

> *"Tell the Dropbox beta story -- Drew showing it to Paul Graham, two days before demo day."*

If you write the whole story in the notes, you'll read it. A single evocative line retrieves the memory. Trust yourself.

---

### Type 4: The Timing Note
Use on slides where pacing matters -- usually long sections, demos, or work-time slides in workshops.

Format: `[2 min]` or `[hold 30 sec]` or `[audience activity -- wait for quiet]`.

This is especially useful in collaborative decks where multiple speakers share a single deck and need to respect each other's time blocks.

---

## What Notes Should Never Be

**Not a script.**
If you write full paragraphs, you'll read them. Reading notes is worse than reading slides -- at least the audience can see the slides. Notes are for *you*, not for the audience, which means they should be short enough to glance at, not read.

**Not a safety net for under-prepared content.**
Notes that say "explain the whole business model here" are a flag that the slide needs to be restructured, not that the notes need to be longer. If a slide requires 400 words of notes to explain, the slide is doing the wrong job.

**Not redundant with the slide.**
If the slide says "40% reduction in churn" and the note says "mention that churn went down 40%" -- delete the note. The slide is already doing that job.

---

## The Relationship Between Slide and Note

Think of it as a two-part system:

| Slide shows | Note carries |
|---|---|
| The visual anchor (image, number, chart) | Why this anchor matters |
| The keyword or concept | The story or context behind it |
| The quote | The moment or person behind the quote |
| The data | The implication of the data |
| The section header | The transition into this section |

The slide earns the audience's attention. The notes earn their understanding.

---

## Writing Notes for the Six Story Arcs

Each arc has predictable moments where notes do the most work:

**Pixar Story Spine:**
Write notes at the "Because of that..." chain. Each link needs to land before the next is triggered. Notes: *"Pause here. Let the consequence land."*

**Hero's Journey:**
Write heavy notes at the Ordeal. This is where speakers rush -- they're uncomfortable with the failure moment. The note should remind you to slow down, not speed up. *"Don't rush through the hard months. This is where they decide if they trust you."*

**Man in a Hole:**
Write notes at the bottom of the hole. The instinct is to get to recovery fast. Resist. The depth of the hole determines the satisfaction of the climb. Note: *"Name the lowest point specifically. Don't soften it."*

**The Quest:**
Write notes at each road test. Each obstacle should feel real, not like a checklist. Notes: *"This one actually threatened the company. Make sure they feel that."*

**Rebirth:**
Write notes at the Catalyst moment. This is the pivot of the whole talk. Notes: *"The specific person / idea / conversation that cracked it. Don't generalize."*

**PAS:**
Write notes at the Agitation beat. Agitation is the hardest to deliver without sounding manipulative. Notes: *"State this calmly. The gravity comes from the truth, not from your intensity."*

## The Opening and Closing Notes

**Opening note (for slide 1):**
Write the exact first sentence you'll say. Not the whole intro -- just the first sentence. Starting strong prevents the "uh, so, yeah, hi everyone..." opener that leaks confidence before you've said anything.

> *"I want to start with a question that still bothers me."*
> *"Three years ago I made a decision that almost ended the company."*
> *"You've already seen the number on this slide. I want to tell you what it cost us to find it."*

**Closing note (for last slide):**
Write the last sentence before you stop talking. This prevents the trailing close ("So... yeah, that's it, any questions?"). The last sentence should feel like a door closing, not a voice trailing off.

> *"The system is already built. The only question is whether you use it."*
> *"That's the bet. We're making it. You're invited."*
> *"I don't know if this is the answer. But I know we're finally asking the right question."*

---

## Formatting Conventions

For consistency across decks, use these conventions in the notes field:

- `[STORY: ...]` -- story prompt (triggers a specific narrative)
- `[PAUSE]` -- deliberate hold; let the idea land
- `[ASK: ...]` -- a question to pose to the audience
- `[DATA: ...]` -- a specific number to state aloud (not shown on slide)
- `[2 MIN]` -- time target for this slide
- `[TRANSITION: ...]` -- exact bridge sentence to next slide

These don't have to be used on every slide -- only where the moment genuinely needs the flag.
